A Multimedia Font Creation Story


You’ve heard of Helvetica, Times, and plenty others, but how many of these fonts fought Nazis? Futura was released in Germany in 1927. Five years later the typeface and its designer Paul Renner took an early and literal stand against the Third Reich in their 1932 booklet Kulturbolschewismus. A year later in 1933 the National Socialists seized control of Germany and Renner was denounced as an intellectually subversive, nationally untrustworthy ‘Cultural Bolshevist’. Within months he was arrested, dismissed from his position as an educator, and forbidden from returning to any regular form of work. Humiliated by this cultural exile, Renner survived by refocusing his efforts towards typography. Two years after the 1939 publication of his book, Die Kunst der Typographie (The Art of Typography), the Nazis, along with much of the world, were printing in Futura.


Decades later, Futura’s popularity soared to cosmic heights. In July of 1969, the Apollo 11 Lunar Landing Mission put Neil Armstrong and Edwin “Buzz” Aldrin on the moon. The plaque on the front landing gear of their lunar module read

HERE MEN FROM THE PLANET EARTH
FIRST SET FOOT UPON THE MOON
JULY 1969, A.D.
WE CAME IN PEACE FOR ALL MANKIND

It was printed in Futura.


To this day, from Avon to Alfa Romeo and Absolut Vodka to Volkswagen, Futura remains iconic and is among the most used commercial typefaces. With its nondecorative, geometric sans-serif styling and sharp extended points, it can be used for headlines and body text alike, making it a highly versatile typeface. From its bold beginning standing against Nazism, all the way to the freaking moon and back, Futura presses forward.
